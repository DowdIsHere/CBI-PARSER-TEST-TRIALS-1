# CBI Tennis Parser — Input Asymmetry: Reaction-Map vs. Archive
**V2.1 companion principle. Explains how the Future↔Past collision actually resolves at the level of what each architecture consumes as input. Changes no rule; names a mechanism the collision rules (Section 3) already imply.**

---

## The principle

Future and Past run on different raw material, and both run continuously.

- **Future builds a reaction-map.** It feeds inputs and watches outputs — *when I do this, the opponent reacts like this; when I do that, like that.* It builds a model of which input produces which reaction, then projects forward from the map. Its raw material is **response behavior.**
- **Past builds an archive.** It collects confirmed, repeatable patterns and exploits the record. Its raw material is **patterns.**

This asymmetry is the engine underneath the Section 3 line "Past can catch Future only when Future leaves enough history." The question is always: *whose behavior is becoming the history?*

---

## Parsers are on 24/7

A Parser is not a mode that switches on when a player is comfortable and off under pressure. The orientation runs continuously — ahead or behind, winning or losing the point. This has three consequences that reads routinely miss:

1. **A Future Parser does not have to lose to collect.** Collection is not a cost paid by dropping early games. It is simply what the architecture does on every exchange, outcome-independent. Down 2-0 or up 2-0, the reaction-map is being written either way. The score early is noise *to the Future player* because the map doesn't care who won the point — only that the exchange happened and a reaction was observed.

2. **A "collapse" is often a map finishing, not a momentum swing.** When a Future player suddenly runs away with a set after a competitive one, the likely event is not "they found rhythm" — it is that the reaction-map became complete enough to project from. Points stop being contests once the reader can predict the reaction to every input. They are not hitting harder; they know where the opponent will be.

3. **A Past player's early success can be the exact mechanism of their defeat.** Against a Future reader, early wins are not a lead being established — they are response-data being collected. If the Past player reads those early wins as a *confirmed pattern* and repeats what worked, they feed the map that defeats them. The more they lean on "what worked," the more legible they become to a player who wins by reading legibility.

---

## The experience tell

A seasoned Past player feels the study happening — knows that being ahead against a Future reader means *you are being mapped right now*, and that repeating a small early sample is handing over response-consistency. An inexperienced Past player mistakes the early sample for a confirmed archive, repeats it, and does not realize their own reactions are the pattern being written.

So: **a Past player who treats an early sample as a confirmed pattern, and repeats it against a Future player, is leaving response-history — not building an archive.** That is an inexperience signature, and it is a falsification signal for any read that had the Past player catching the Future player.

---

## The falsification watch

For a Future vs. Past read, watch legibility direction, not the scoreboard:

- Is the Past player becoming **more predictable** as the match runs (same responses to the same situations)? → the map is closing → the collision is turning toward Future, regardless of set score.
- Is the Past player **repeating early-success behavior** rather than confirming a genuinely stable opponent pattern? → they are feeding the map.
- Do the **deuce / pressure points** start going the reader's way after a competitive early phase? → the map has closed enough to project under pressure. This is the tell that the resolution has arrived.

A single break either way is still noise (Section 6). The signal is the legibility *trend*, read across games.

---

## Why this is a Profile → Conditions → Parser instance, not an exception

Per the Ordering: the Parser is the output of Profile through Conditions. The reaction-map is how a Future Parser's output updates *over time* as it accumulates input — it is the Parser doing its job continuously, not a separate force. The stats that measure it (legibility trend, situation recurrence, pressure-point drift) are the **instrument** that tells you how far the resolution has progressed — they are not a competing input and never the verdict. The verdict remains the Parser read; the instrument tells you where in its lifecycle the collision is.

This is the lesson the W15 Tokyo match actually taught — not an environment lesson. The fast court did not shut the Past player's path (she led 2-0 and 3-2). She was mapped, repeated what worked, and the map closed. Reach for input-asymmetry before reaching for environment when a Past player's early lead evaporates against a Future reader.
